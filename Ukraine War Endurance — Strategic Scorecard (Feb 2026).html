Ukraine War Endurance — Strategic Scorecard

Economic and Strategic Sustainability Assessment
Update: February 2026
Observational • Non-Forecast

Russia — Endurance Under Margin Compression

Current Condition

  • Oil export volumes continue, but netbacks are structurally compressed.
  • Fiscal buffers are eroding gradually rather than collapsing.
  • Buyer optionality has narrowed as India becomes less predictable.
  • China now dominates marginal demand and pricing leverage.
  • Labor scarcity and defense-industry strain raise the cost per unit of military output.

Assessment: Russia can sustain the war, but each additional quarter requires greater economic concession and rising inefficiency. Endurance exists; resilience does not.

India — From Arbiter to Conditional Buyer

Current Condition

  • Russian crude is no longer foundational to supply security.
  • Imports diversified toward U.S., Middle Eastern, and licensed Venezuelan barrels.
  • Purchasing behavior is more selective and less predictable.

Assessment: India does not need to exit Russian oil to exert influence. By reducing pricing reliability, it tightens Russia’s margins while preserving strategic autonomy.

China — Marginal Buyer, Maximum Leverage

Current Condition

  • Only buyer with meaningful scale elasticity for Russian crude.
  • Absorbs barrels opportunistically, not contractually.
  • Demands deeper discounts as Russia’s alternatives shrink.

Assessment: China enables continuation of Russian exports while pricing dependence. This is leverage, not partnership.

Ukraine — Endurance via External Alignment

Current Condition

  • Military and economic endurance remain externally funded.
  • Western financial and defense support is institutionalized near-term.
  • Russia’s margin compression indirectly supports Ukraine’s attrition strategy.

Assessment: Ukraine’s endurance is constrained less by economics than by the continuity of external political alignment.

Board-Level Takeaway

The Ukraine war is no longer constrained by oil volumes, but by oil economics. As India reduces its role as a balancing buyer, China increasingly prices Russia’s endurance — forcing Moscow to trade long-term resilience and autonomy for short-term military continuity.

Author's Takeaway

I’ve been tracking how sanctions are affecting Russia and developed a Russia-focused scorecard model, which I ran using both ChatGPT and Copilot. The above analysis reflects the ChatGPT results, which were more cautious than Copilot about the likelihood of India ending imports of Russian crude. In my view, the issue largely comes down to economics. If India can secure comparable discounts by buying oil from Venezuela or other suppliers, shifting away from Russian oil would make economic sense. Once India clearly commits one way or another—setting political rhetoric aside—I’ll rerun the model to assess what has changed.
